* * * SKETCHES * * *

First thing: the idea. To create the project, I was torn between a vintage computer or a sacred object. So I decided to go for something in between that I could make by myself. I sketched out various designs, including crucifixes, monitors, and angelic figures with wings made of computer chips. Drawing inspiration from urban dioramas and chip art, I chose to build a church using the most important parts of old computers, taking Notre Dame as the model where possible.

------------------

* * * SCULPTURE * * *

After several attempts, I finally finished the sculpture using symbolic parts like monitors, old computer cases, and keyboards, but not only those. I also used numerous floppy disks as decorations and flying buttresses, mouses, motherboards, and a mysterious object I found nearby (probably some kind of mixer) that I used as a spire.

__________________

* * * THE CLOUD * * *

Before taking the photos, I waited for optimal environmental conditions, which, in this case, were early mornings or late afternoons. The sculpture had various transparent and reflective objects, fortunately covered in dust. I also equipped myself with talcum powder and applied it where possible. Using a Fujifilm X30 camera, I took a total of 404 photos of the subject, which I later loaded into Agisoft Metashape software. After a laborious process of cropping each individual image, there was an alignment phase (lasting approximately 3 hours), followed by an assessment of image quality (during which 4 photos were removed), and a digital measurement phase using markers.

------------------

* * * MESH, TEXTURE and CORRECTION WITH BLENDER * * *

The next phase was quite lengthy. Metashape had generated a point cloud with approximately 270,000 points; it was time to enclose the subject within the safe area of the bounding box and perform point cleaning. Once that was completed, I generated the mesh (which took about 6 hours) and calibrated the colors. Finally, after an entire night of texturing, I loaded the model into Blender to fix some imperfections and add the decorative base.

At the end of it all, I exported the .blend file to .obj and used MeshLab to convert it to .ply. I then used Nexus to convert it to a multiresolution (.nxs) and compressed (.nxz) file before uploading it to the web using 3DHop.
